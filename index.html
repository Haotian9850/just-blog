As a Collegiate Cyclist

I am a driven twenty-year-old student of the University of Virginia grown up in Guangzhou, China.

Having always fascinated by the immense possibitliy space offers and the prospect of a multi-panetary civilization, I chose to study Astronomy and Computer Engineering (later Computer Science) at the University of Virginia, the first American University that offered bachelor's degrees in Astronomy in Charlottesville, Central Virginia.

Concurrent to my rapidly broadening knowledge and passion for my fields of studies was an abrupt transformation of mind. Experiencing the drastic cultural shift from China to the U.S. at the age of 17, I suddenly became afraid of being “just” one of the many Asian students residing in the United States, who, for conceivable reasons, have yet to escaped from largely negative media portrayal and the seemingly harmless classification of "model minority".

Struggling to find myself a true identity, I picked up an Asian American history class, spent some sleepless nights in Kellogg, and, at some point, decided to try road cycling. Don’t get me wrong-I was never considered an athlete under any circumstances or someone with exceptional physical capabilities. Nor does my family have a strong background in any sport or traditions to support a athletic son. While I had fallen in love with bikes at a very young age, my scope of riding was limited to just middle school commuting, some long-distance weekend rides, and one local teenager mountain bike race.

Nevertheless, road cycling has became a somewhat central part to how I now perceive myself. The unique cycling scene in Charlottesville and broader Atlantic region has helped elevate me from an absolute road newbie to a somewhat ambitious cyclist. After some great but brief time racing in the ACCC (Atlantic Collegiate Cycling Conference) and constant friend-making, I became determined to pursue higher categories in this sport, and move up the ladder as much as possible.

Some may question my motive: after all, I might never attempt to claim to be specially talented in this sport and reach the professional rank - so why bother spending so much time and resources? To a certain degree, these doubts are true. But perhaps more importantly, entering the sport was where I learned the benefits of persistence, self-discipline and openmindedness. Through pedaling, I have gained the ability to be dedicated to one thing and defy some of my deepest concerns and fears. To this end, my cycling career has helped me a lot, and I’ll be more than happy to just keep it happening.
